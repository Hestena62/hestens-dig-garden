# Study Guide: Anatomy and Physiology of the Cardiovascular System

## Quiz: Short-Answer Questions

_Instructions: Answer the following questions in 2-3 complete sentences, drawing upon your understanding of the provided materials._

1. Describe the primary function of the cardiovascular system and its main components.
2. Explain the structural differences between an artery and a vein, particularly regarding the tunica media.
3. Trace the path of deoxygenated blood through the pulmonary circuit, starting from the right ventricle and ending at the left atrium.
4. What do the systolic and diastolic numbers in a blood pressure reading represent?
5. Describe the three layers of the heart wall and the protective sac that encloses the heart.
6. What events in the cardiac cycle cause the characteristic "lub-dub" sounds of the heartbeat?
7. Explain the function of capillaries and why their structure is uniquely suited for this role.
8. Define atherosclerosis and explain how it can lead to a myocardial infarction (heart attack).
9. What is the purpose of the coronary arteries, and why is a blockage in them so dangerous?
10. Describe the procedure known as balloon angioplasty and the role a stent plays in it.

## Answer Key

1. The cardiovascular system's primary function is to transport materials to and from the body's cells to maintain homeostasis. Its main components are the heart, which pumps blood; the blood vessels (arteries, veins, capillaries), which contain the blood; and the blood itself, which carries oxygen, nutrients, hormones, and waste products.
2. Arteries and veins both have three layers (tunica intima, media, and externa), but the tunica media in an artery is much thicker and more elastic. This is because arteries carry high-pressure blood away from the heart and need to withstand that force, while veins carry low-pressure blood toward the heart and have a thinner muscle layer. Veins also contain valves to prevent backflow, which arteries do not.
3. Deoxygenated blood is pumped from the right ventricle through the pulmonary semilunar valve into the pulmonary trunk and pulmonary arteries, which carry it to the lungs. In the lung capillaries, the blood releases carbon dioxide and picks up oxygen. This newly oxygenated blood then returns to the left atrium of the heart via the pulmonary veins.
4. Systolic pressure, the higher number, represents the maximum pressure exerted on artery walls when the ventricles contract (systole). Diastolic pressure, the lower number, is the pressure on the artery walls when the ventricles are relaxed and filling with blood (diastole).
5. The heart wall consists of three layers: the inner endocardium, the thick muscular myocardium that performs the pumping, and the outer epicardium. The heart is enclosed in a protective sac called the pericardium, which has a fibrous outer layer and an inner serous layer that contains fluid to reduce friction and protect from shock.
6. The first sound, "lub," is caused by the closing of the atrioventricular (AV) valves (the tricuspid and mitral valves) at the beginning of ventricular contraction (systole). The second sound, "dub," is caused by the closing of the semilunar (aortic and pulmonary) valves as the ventricles relax (diastole).
7. Capillaries are the site of exchange between the blood and the body's tissues. Their structure consists of only a single, thin layer of endothelium (tunica intima), which makes them perfectly suited for the diffusion of substances like oxygen, carbon dioxide, nutrients, and wastes between the blood and the surrounding interstitial fluid.
8. Atherosclerosis is the buildup of fatty deposits, cholesterol, and other substances into growths called plaques on the inner walls of arteries. If a plaque in a coronary artery ruptures, it can trigger the formation of a blood clot (thrombus) that causes a complete blockage (occlusion), stopping blood flow to a section of the heart muscle and causing the tissue to die.
9. The coronary arteries are the first branches off the aorta, and their purpose is to supply the heart muscle (myocardium) itself with oxygen-rich blood. A blockage in these arteries is extremely dangerous because it deprives the hard-working heart muscle of the oxygen it needs to function, leading to cell death, which is a heart attack.
10. Balloon angioplasty is a procedure to treat clogged arteries where a catheter with a small balloon at its tip is guided to the blockage. The balloon is then inflated, crushing the plaque against the artery wall and widening the lumen to improve blood flow. A stent, which is a small mesh tube, is often left in place to hold the artery open and prevent it from re-narrowing.

## Glossary of Key Terms

|   |   |
|---|---|
|Term|Definition|
|**Aneurysm**|A condition where an artery wall weakens and balloons out, making it vulnerable to rupture.|
|**Angina Pectoris**|Strong, heavy pressure or pain in the chest, shoulder, or arm, often a warning sign of a partially blocked coronary artery.|
|**Angioplasty**|A medical procedure used to open clogged arteries, often involving a balloon to crush plaque and a stent to keep the vessel open.|
|**Aorta**|The largest artery in the body, which carries oxygenated blood from the left ventricle to the systemic circuit.|
|**Arteriole**|A small-diameter blood vessel that branches out from an artery and leads to capillaries. Arterioles have smooth muscle walls capable of vasoconstriction and vasodilation to regulate blood pressure.|
|**Arteriosclerosis**|A condition often occurring with atherosclerosis where plaques in the artery become hardened due to calcium deposits, leading to "hardening of the arteries."|
|**Artery**|A blood vessel that carries blood away from the heart. Arteries typically have thick, muscular, and elastic walls to withstand high pressure.|
|**Atherosclerosis**|The buildup of fatty deposits and cholesterol into growths called plaques on the inner walls of arteries, narrowing the vessel.|
|**Atrium (pl. Atria)**|One of the two upper, thin-walled chambers of the heart that receive blood returning to the heart. The right atrium receives deoxygenated blood; the left atrium receives oxygenated blood.|
|**Atrioventricular (AV) Valves**|The valves located between the atria and the ventricles. These include the tricuspid valve (right side) and the bicuspid/mitral valve (left side).|
|**Bicuspid Valve**|Also known as the mitral valve, it is the AV valve with two cusps located between the left atrium and the left ventricle.|
|**Blood Pressure**|The force exerted by circulating blood on the walls of blood vessels. It is typically measured in the brachial artery and expressed as systolic pressure over diastolic pressure.|
|**Capillary**|The smallest and thinnest blood vessels, consisting of a single layer of endothelium. They form networks (capillary beds) where the exchange of gases, nutrients, and waste products occurs between blood and body tissues.|
|**Cardiac Arrest**|A condition that occurs when the heart's electrical system malfunctions, and the heart muscle fails to contract correctly, stopping blood flow to the brain and body.|
|**Cardiac Catheterization**|A procedure where a thin, hollow tube (catheter) is inserted into a blood vessel and guided to the heart to diagnose and sometimes treat heart conditions.|
|**Cardiac Cycle**|The sequence of events that occurs during one heartbeat, consisting of a contraction phase (systole) and a relaxation phase (diastole). A normal cycle lasts about 0.85 seconds.|
|**Cardiac Output**|The volume of blood pumped by the heart per minute. It is calculated as heart rate (beats per minute) multiplied by stroke volume (volume pumped per beat).|
|**Chordae Tendineae**|Tendon-like, fibrous cords ("heart strings") that attach the cusps of the AV valves to the papillary muscles in the ventricles, preventing the valves from inverting during ventricular contraction.|
|**Coronary Arteries**|The blood vessels that branch off the aorta to supply the heart muscle (myocardium) itself with oxygenated blood.|
|**Diastole**|The phase of the cardiac cycle when the heart chambers (especially the ventricles) are relaxing and filling with blood.|
|**Diastolic Pressure**|The lowest pressure in the arteries, occurring during the relaxation phase (diastole) of the ventricles.|
|**Echocardiogram**|An ultrasound of the heart that uses sound waves to create live images of the heart's chambers, valves, and pumping action.|
|**Embolus**|A blood clot (or other mass) that has broken free from its point of origin and is traveling through the bloodstream. An embolus can lodge in a smaller vessel and cause a blockage.|
|**Endocardium**|The innermost, thin layer of tissue that lines the chambers and valves of the heart.|
|**Epicardium**|The outermost layer of the heart wall, also known as the visceral pericardium. It houses the coronary arteries and veins.|
|**Hypertension**|High blood pressure, medically defined as a persistent blood pressure of 130/80 mmHg or higher.|
|**Inferior Vena Cava**|The large vein that carries deoxygenated blood from the lower and middle body into the right atrium of the heart.|
|**Lumen**|The central space or cavity within a blood vessel through which blood flows.|
|**Mitral Valve**|See **Bicuspid Valve**.|
|**Myocardial Infarction**|The medical term for a heart attack, which is the death of cardiac muscle tissue resulting from a prolonged blockage of one or more coronary arteries.|
|**Myocardium**|The thick middle layer of the heart wall, composed of cardiac muscle, which is responsible for the heart's pumping action.|
|**Papillary Muscles**|Small muscles located on the inner walls of the ventricles that contract to pull on the chordae tendineae, preventing the AV valves from prolapsing into the atria.|
|**Pericardium**|The protective, double-layered sac that encloses the heart. It consists of an outer fibrous layer and an inner serous layer with fluid that reduces friction.|
|**Plaque**|A deposit of fat, cholesterol, calcium, and other substances that builds up inside an artery wall, characteristic of atherosclerosis.|
|**Precapillary Sphincters**|Rings of smooth muscle at the junction of arterioles and capillaries that can contract or relax to control and regulate blood flow into a capillary bed.|
|**Pulmonary Circuit**|The circulatory pathway that carries deoxygenated blood from the right ventricle to the lungs and returns oxygenated blood to the left atrium.|
|**Pulse**|The rhythmic expansion and recoil of an artery wall that can be felt, caused by the surge of blood pushed from the heart during ventricular contraction. It is an indirect measure of heart rate.|
|**Semilunar Valves**|The valves located at the exit of each ventricle, shaped like half-moons. They include the pulmonary semilunar valve (from right ventricle to pulmonary artery) and the aortic semilunar valve (from left ventricle to aorta).|
|**Septum**|The muscular wall that divides the heart into right and left sides, preventing the mixing of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood.|
|**Sphygmomanometer**|The medical device, also known as a blood pressure cuff, used to measure blood pressure.|
|**Stent**|A small, expandable mesh tube placed inside a clogged artery during angioplasty to keep the artery open and maintain blood flow.|
|**Stroke**|The death of nervous tissue in the brain, usually resulting from a blockage (caused by a thrombus or embolus) or rupture of an artery that supplies the brain.|
|**Stroke Volume**|The volume of blood pumped from one ventricle of the heart with each beat. The average is approximately 70 mL.|
|**Superior Vena Cava**|The large vein that carries deoxygenated blood from the upper body (head, neck, arms) into the right atrium of the heart.|
|**Systemic Circuit**|The circulatory pathway that carries oxygenated blood from the left ventricle to all body tissues and returns deoxygenated blood to the right atrium.|
|**Systole**|The phase of the cardiac cycle when the heart chambers (especially the ventricles) are contracting and pumping blood out.|
|**Systolic Pressure**|The highest pressure in the arteries, occurring during the contraction phase (systole) of the ventricles.|
|**Thrombus**|A stationary blood clot that forms inside a blood vessel or the heart, obstructing blood flow at its point of formation.|
|**Thorax**|The part of the body between the neck and the abdomen, also known as the chest. The heart is located in the thoracic cavity.|
|**Tricuspid Valve**|The AV valve with three cusps located between the right atrium and the right ventricle.|
|**Tunica Externa**|Also called tunica adventitia, it is the outermost layer of a blood vessel, composed of connective tissue (mostly collagen) that provides structural support.|
|**Tunica Intima**|The innermost layer of a blood vessel, composed of a single layer of endothelium cells that are in direct contact with the blood.|
|**Tunica Media**|The middle layer of a blood vessel, composed of smooth muscle and elastic fibers. It is responsible for changing the diameter of the vessel.|
|**Vasa Vasorum**|A network of small blood vessels that supply the walls of large blood vessels, such as the aorta, with nourishment.|
|**Vein**|A blood vessel that carries blood toward the heart. Veins typically have thinner walls, a larger lumen, and one-way valves to prevent the backflow of low-pressure blood.|
|**Ventricle**|One of the two lower, thick-walled chambers of the heart that pump blood away from the heart. The right ventricle pumps to the lungs; the left ventricle pumps to the rest of the body.|
|**Venule**|A small blood vessel that collects blood from the capillaries and drains it into a vein.|
