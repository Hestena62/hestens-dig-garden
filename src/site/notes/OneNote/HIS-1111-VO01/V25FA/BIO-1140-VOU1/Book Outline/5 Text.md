Section 5.1: Overview of the Cardiovascular System  
The cardiovascular system includes the heart and blood vessels.  
Its main function is to transport oxygen, nutrients, hormones, and waste products.  
It works closely with other systems (respiratory, digestive, urinary).  
The lymphatic system supports circulation by returning excess fluid to the bloodstream.
 
Section 5.2: Types of Blood Vessels  
Arteries carry blood away from the heart; they have thick walls to withstand pressure.  
Capillaries are tiny vessels where exchange of gases, nutrients, and wastes occurs.  
Veins return blood to the heart; they have valves to prevent backflow and act as blood reservoirs.
 
Section 5.3: The Heart as a Double Pump  
The heart has four chambers: right/left atria and right/left ventricles.  
Blood flows in a double circuit: pulmonary (to lungs) and systemic (to body).  
Valves ensure one-way blood flow.  
The cardiac cycle includes systole (contraction) and diastole (relaxation).  
The SA node initiates the heartbeat; the AV node coordinates ventricular contraction.  
ECG records electrical activity of the heart.
 
Section 5.4: Blood Pressure  
Pulse reflects heart rate.  
Blood pressure is highest in arteries and lowest in veins.  
Systolic pressure is during heart contraction; diastolic is during relaxation.  
Veins rely on skeletal muscle pumps, respiratory pumps, and valves for blood return.
 
Section 5.5: Two Cardiovascular Pathways  
Pulmonary circuit: right side of heart → lungs → left side of heart.  
Systemic circuit: left side of heart → body → right side of heart.  
Hepatic portal system: transports nutrient-rich blood from digestive tract to liver for processing.
 
Section 5.6: Exchange at the Capillaries  
Blood pressure pushes fluid out of capillaries; osmotic pressure pulls it back in.  
Most fluid returns to capillaries; excess is collected by the lymphatic system as lymph.
 
Section 5.7: Cardiovascular Disorders  
Hypertension (high blood pressure) and atherosclerosis (plaque buildup) are major risks.  
Can lead to stroke, heart attack, aneurysm, and angina.  
Treatments include medications, angioplasty, bypass surgery, stents, and artificial hearts.  
Lifestyle changes (diet, exercise, avoiding smoking) are key to prevention.
