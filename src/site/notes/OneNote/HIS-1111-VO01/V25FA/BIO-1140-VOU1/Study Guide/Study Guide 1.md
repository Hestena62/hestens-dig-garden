## Quiz Answer Key

1. **Compare and contrast the general characteristics and response times of the endocrine and nervous systems.** Both the nervous and endocrine systems use chemical signals for communication. The nervous system is organized for a rapid response using neurotransmitters across a short synapse, which is ideal for immediate actions like avoiding danger. The endocrine system functions more slowly, using hormones that travel through the bloodstream to reach target cells, resulting in a longer-lasting effect.
2. **Explain the primary difference between endocrine and exocrine glands, providing an example of each type.** The primary difference lies in where they secrete their products. Endocrine glands are ductless and secrete hormones directly into the bloodstream to travel throughout the body; the pituitary gland is an example. Exocrine glands secrete their products through ducts to a specific location, often to the "outside" or into a cavity; examples include salivary glands secreting saliva into the mouth or sweat glands.
3. **Describe the mechanism of action for a steroid hormone after it reaches its target cell. What makes this mechanism possible?** Steroid hormones are lipid-soluble, allowing them to easily diffuse across the target cell's plasma membrane. Once inside, they bind to a receptor protein in the cytoplasm or nucleus, and this hormone-receptor complex then interacts directly with the cell's DNA. This interaction activates specific genes, leading to the transcription of mRNA and the synthesis of new proteins that alter the cell's metabolism.
4. **What is signal transduction, and why is it necessary for non-steroid hormones to exert their effects on a target cell?** Signal transduction is the process by which a message from a hormone is relayed through a series of secondary messengers within a cell to cause a response. This is necessary for non-steroid hormones (like peptides and proteins) because they cannot pass through the cell membrane. The hormone (the first messenger) binds to a receptor on the cell surface, triggering a cascade of intracellular events involving second messengers like cyclic AMP (cAMP) to ultimately alter cell function.
5. **Explain the role of the hypothalamus in controlling the anterior pituitary gland. What type of hormones does it use for this regulation?** The hypothalamus acts as a critical link between the nervous and endocrine systems, controlling the glandular secretions of the anterior pituitary. It produces and secretes specific hypothalamic-releasing and hypothalamic-inhibiting hormones. These hormones travel through a portal system of blood vessels directly to the anterior pituitary, stimulating or inhibiting the release of its various hormones.
6. **Identify the two hormones released by the posterior pituitary gland and describe one primary function for each.** The posterior pituitary releases Antidiuretic Hormone (ADH) and Oxytocin, both of which are produced by neurosecretory cells in the hypothalamus. ADH targets the kidneys, causing them to reabsorb more water and reduce urine output, thereby regulating the body's water balance. Oxytocin stimulates uterine muscle contractions during childbirth and also triggers the release of milk from the mammary glands.
7. **How do the hormones calcitonin and parathyroid hormone (PTH) work together to regulate blood calcium levels?** Calcitonin and PTH are antagonists that maintain calcium homeostasis. When blood calcium is high, the thyroid gland releases calcitonin, which lowers calcium levels by stimulating its storage in bones. Conversely, when blood calcium is low, the parathyroid glands release PTH, which raises calcium levels by stimulating its release from bones, increasing its reabsorption by the kidneys, and promoting its absorption in the intestines.
8. **Describe the "fight-or-flight" response, identifying the gland and specific hormones involved in this short-term stress reaction.** The "fight-or-flight" response is a rapid, short-term reaction to an emergency situation, initiated by nerve signals from the sympathetic nervous system. These signals stimulate the adrenal medulla to secrete epinephrine (adrenaline) and norepinephrine. These hormones increase heart rate, blood pressure, and blood glucose levels while shunting blood to the heart, brain, and skeletal muscles to prepare the body for immediate action.
9. **Explain the antagonistic relationship between insulin and glucagon in maintaining blood glucose homeostasis.** Insulin and glucagon, both produced by the pancreas, have opposing effects on blood sugar. After a meal when blood glucose is high, beta cells release insulin, which stimulates liver, muscle, and adipose cells to take up glucose, lowering blood levels. When blood glucose is low, alpha cells release glucagon, which stimulates the liver to break down stored glycogen into glucose and release it into the blood, raising blood levels.
10. **What is a goiter, and what dietary deficiency is most commonly responsible for its development?** A goiter is an enlargement of the thyroid gland, often visible as a swelling in the neck. It is most commonly caused by a dietary deficiency of iodine. Without sufficient iodine, the thyroid cannot produce its hormones (T3 and T4), leading the pituitary to continuously secrete Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone (TSH) in an attempt to stimulate the gland, causing it to enlarge.

## Glossary of Key Terms

|   |   |
|---|---|
|Term|Definition|
|**Acromegaly**|A condition caused by the overproduction of growth hormone in adults, leading to the enlargement of bones in the face, hands, and feet.|
|**Addison's Disease**|A disorder caused by hyposecretion of glucocorticoids and mineralocorticoids from the adrenal cortex, characterized by bronzing of the skin and metabolic disturbances.|
|**Adenylate Cyclase**|An enzyme activated by a G protein in the signal transduction pathway of non-steroid hormones. It converts ATP to cyclic AMP (cAMP).|
|**Adrenal Cortex**|The outer portion of the adrenal gland that produces long-term stress response hormones, including glucocorticoids (like cortisol) and mineralocorticoids (like aldosterone), as well as sex hormones.|
|**Adrenal Gland**|An endocrine gland located on top of each kidney, composed of an outer cortex and an inner medulla, which together regulate stress responses and electrolyte balance.|
|**Adrenal Medulla**|The inner portion of the adrenal gland, controlled by the nervous system, that secretes epinephrine and norepinephrine to produce the short-term "fight-or-flight" response.|
|**Adrenocorticotropic Hormone (ACTH)**|A hormone released by the anterior pituitary that stimulates the adrenal cortex to secrete its hormones, particularly glucocorticoids like cortisol.|
|**Aldosterone**|A mineralocorticoid hormone produced by the adrenal cortex that regulates electrolyte balance by causing the kidneys to conserve sodium and excrete potassium.|
|**Androgens**|Male sex hormones, such as testosterone, produced primarily by the testes and in small amounts by the adrenal cortex.|
|**Antagonist**|A substance or hormone that acts in opposition to another. For example, insulin and glucagon are antagonists in regulating blood sugar.|
|**Anterior Pituitary**|The front lobe of the pituitary gland, which produces and secretes six major hormones: GH, Prolactin, TSH, ACTH, FSH, and LH. Its secretions are controlled by the hypothalamus.|
|**Antidiuretic Hormone (ADH)**|A hormone produced by the hypothalamus and released by the posterior pituitary that stimulates water reabsorption by the kidneys.|
|**Calcitonin**|A hormone produced by the thyroid gland that lowers high blood calcium levels by stimulating calcium deposition in bones and teeth.|
|**Cortisol**|A primary glucocorticoid produced by the adrenal cortex in response to stress. It raises blood glucose levels, promotes the breakdown of muscle protein, and has an anti-inflammatory effect.|
|**Cushing's Syndrome**|A disorder resulting from excessive levels of glucocorticoids, which can be caused by tumors. Symptoms include muscle protein breakdown and a redistribution of fat.|
|**Cyclic Adenosine Monophosphate (cAMP)**|A common second messenger in the signal transduction pathway for non-steroid hormones. It activates protein kinases, leading to a cellular response.|
|**Diabetes Insipidus**|A condition caused by an inability to produce ADH, resulting in excessive urination and dehydration.|
|**Diabetes Mellitus**|A group of diseases characterized by high blood glucose levels resulting from defects in insulin production (Type 1), insulin action (Type 2), or both.|
|**Endocrine Gland**|A ductless gland that secretes hormones directly into the bloodstream. Examples include the pituitary, thyroid, and adrenal glands.|
|**Epinephrine (Adrenaline)**|A hormone secreted by the adrenal medulla during the "fight-or-flight" response that increases heart rate, blood pressure, and blood glucose.|
|**Erythropoietin (EPO)**|A hormone produced by the kidneys in response to low blood oxygen that stimulates the production of red blood cells in the bone marrow.|
|**Estrogen**|A primary female sex hormone produced by the ovaries and adrenal cortex that stimulates the development of female secondary sex characteristics and regulates the uterine cycle.|
|**Exocrine Gland**|A gland that releases its secretions through ducts into a body cavity or onto a surface. Examples include salivary, sweat, and mammary glands.|
|**Follicle-Stimulating Hormone (FSH)**|A gonadotropin from the anterior pituitary that stimulates egg development in the ovaries and sperm production in the testes.|
|**Gigantism**|A condition caused by excessive production of growth hormone during childhood, leading to abnormally large stature.|
|**Gland**|A group of specialized cells that produce and secrete substances, such as hormones.|
|**Glucagon**|A hormone secreted by the alpha cells of the pancreas that raises low blood glucose levels by stimulating the liver to break down glycogen and release glucose.|
|**Glucocorticoids**|A class of steroid hormones from the adrenal cortex, including cortisol, that regulate carbohydrate, protein, and fat metabolism and have anti-inflammatory effects.|
|**Goiter**|An enlargement of the thyroid gland, typically resulting from an iodine deficiency.|
|**Gonadotropins**|Hormones that target the gonads (testes and ovaries), specifically FSH and LH.|
|**Gonads**|The primary reproductive organs (testes in males, ovaries in females) that produce gametes and sex hormones.|
|**Graves' Disease**|A common form of hyperthyroidism, an autoimmune disorder that results in overactivity of the thyroid gland and symptoms like bulging eyes (exophthalmos).|
|**Growth Hormone (GH)**|A protein hormone from the anterior pituitary that stimulates growth, especially of bones and muscles, and affects metabolism.|
|**Hormone**|A chemical signal or messenger produced by an endocrine gland, transported by the bloodstream, that affects target cells to regulate metabolic activity. The word means "to excite or set into motion."|
|**Hypothalamus**|A region of the brain below the thalamus that acts as the link between the nervous and endocrine systems. It controls the pituitary gland by producing releasing and inhibiting hormones.|
|**Insulin**|A protein hormone secreted by the beta cells of the pancreas that lowers high blood glucose levels by promoting the uptake of glucose by cells.|
|**Islets of Langerhans**|Clusters of endocrine cells within the pancreas that produce insulin (from beta cells) and glucagon (from alpha cells).|
|**Leptin**|A protein hormone produced by adipose tissue that signals the hypothalamus about fat stores, influencing appetite and feelings of fullness.|
|**Luteinizing Hormone (LH)**|A gonadotropin from the anterior pituitary that triggers ovulation and stimulates the corpus luteum in females, and stimulates testosterone production in males.|
|**Melatonin**|A hormone produced by the pineal gland, primarily at night, that regulates circadian rhythms (sleep-wake cycles) and is involved in maturation.|
|**Mineralocorticoids**|A class of steroid hormones from the adrenal cortex, including aldosterone, that regulate the balance of minerals (like sodium and potassium) and water in the body.|
|**Negative Feedback**|A primary control mechanism in which the product of a pathway inhibits an earlier step in the pathway, maintaining homeostasis. Most hormone secretion is regulated this way.|
|**Non-steroid Hormone**|A class of hormones, including peptides and proteins (like insulin), that are not lipid-soluble and therefore bind to receptors on the surface of target cells, requiring second messengers.|
|**Norepinephrine**|A hormone secreted by the adrenal medulla with effects similar to epinephrine, contributing to the "fight-or-flight" response.|
|**Ovaries**|The female gonads, which produce eggs and the female sex hormones estrogen and progesterone.|
|**Oxytocin**|A hormone produced by the hypothalamus and released by the posterior pituitary that stimulates uterine contractions during childbirth and milk release during lactation. Known as a "positive feedback" hormone.|
|**Pancreas**|A gland located behind the stomach with both endocrine (producing insulin and glucagon) and exocrine (producing digestive enzymes) functions.|
|**Parathyroid Glands**|Four small glands located on the posterior surface of the thyroid that secrete parathyroid hormone (PTH).|
|**Parathyroid Hormone (PTH)**|A hormone that raises low blood calcium levels by stimulating its release from bones and increasing its reabsorption by the kidneys and intestines.|
|**Pheromones**|Chemical signals that are released into the environment and influence the behavior or physiology of other individuals of the same species.|
|**Pineal Gland**|A small gland in the brain that produces melatonin.|
|**Pituitary Gland**|A pea-sized gland at the base of the brain, consisting of an anterior and posterior lobe. It is often called the "master gland" as it controls many other endocrine glands.|
|**Positive Feedback**|A control mechanism where the output of a system stimulates the system to further increase the output, such as oxytocin release during labor.|
|**Posterior Pituitary**|The rear lobe of the pituitary gland that stores and releases two hormones produced by the hypothalamus: ADH and oxytocin.|
|**Progesterone**|A female sex hormone produced by the ovaries that helps prepare the uterus for pregnancy by thickening its inner lining.|
|**Prolactin (PRL)**|A hormone from the anterior pituitary that stimulates the mammary glands to produce milk.|
|**Prostaglandins**|Locally produced chemical signals derived from fatty acids that act on nearby tissues. They can relax smooth muscles, influence blood pressure, and cause inflammation.|
|**Receptor**|A protein on or within a target cell that specifically binds to a hormone, initiating a response. The interaction is often compared to a lock and key.|
|**Signal Transduction**|The multi-step process by which a non-steroid hormone binding to a cell-surface receptor triggers a cascade of events inside the cell, leading to a final cellular response.|
|**Steroid Hormone**|A class of lipid-soluble hormones derived from cholesterol (e.g., estrogen, testosterone, cortisol) that can pass through the cell membrane and bind to intracellular receptors to directly affect gene expression.|
|**Target Cell**|A specific cell that has a receptor for a particular hormone.|
|**Testes**|The male gonads, which produce sperm and the male sex hormone testosterone.|
|**Testosterone**|The primary male sex hormone, which promotes the development of male secondary sexual characteristics and sperm production.|
|**Thymus**|A gland located beneath the sternum that is large in childhood and shrinks with age. It produces thymosins, which are essential for the maturation of T lymphocytes (T cells) for the immune system.|
|**Thyroid Gland**|A butterfly-shaped gland in the neck that produces thyroxine (T4), triiodothyronine (T3), and calcitonin. T3 and T4 regulate metabolism, while calcitonin regulates blood calcium.|
|**Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone (TSH)**|A hormone released from the anterior pituitary that stimulates the thyroid gland to produce and release its hormones (T3 and T4).|
|**Thyroxine (T4)**|The main hormone produced by the thyroid gland, containing four iodine atoms, which regulates the body's metabolic rate.|
|**Triiodothyronine (T3)**|A thyroid hormone containing three iodine atoms. It is more potent than T4, and most T4 is converted to T3 in target cells.|
