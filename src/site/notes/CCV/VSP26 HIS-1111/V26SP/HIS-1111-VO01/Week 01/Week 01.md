**Prehistory and the Birth of Civilization**
 
**Main Topics Covered**

- Life before written history
- Hunter‑gatherers versus early farmers
- The origins and impact of agriculture
- How prehistory led to the first civilizations￼
 
**Prehistory Overview**  
Prehistory is the long period of human history before written records. It covers millions of years and shows how humans slowly adapted, innovated, and organized their lives. Historians divide prehistory into three main periods: the Paleolithic, Mesolithic, and Neolithic.
 
Early humans, or hominins, first evolved in Africa. Walking upright freed their hands, allowing them to make tools. Over time, early members of the genus Homo learned to create stone tools, control fire, and spread to other parts of the world. Fire was especially important because it allowed cooking, warmth, safety, and stronger social bonds.￼
 
**Paleolithic Period (Old Stone Age)**  
During the Paleolithic period, humans lived as hunter‑gatherers. They moved in small groups, hunted animals, and gathered wild plants. Their tools slowly improved, including spears and scrapers. Life depended on cooperation, so Paleolithic societies were mostly equal, with little social hierarchy.
 
This period also saw the beginnings of human culture and creativity. Cave paintings and other early art show that people were developing symbolic thinking and spiritual ideas.
 
**Mesolithic Period (Middle Stone Age)**  
The Mesolithic period was a time of transition. As the last Ice Age ended and the climate warmed, humans adapted to new environments. Diets became more diverse, with more fishing and use of nuts and plants.
 
Tools improved with the use of small stone blades called microliths. Some groups began settling in one place part‑time, especially near rivers and coastlines. These changes set the stage for agriculture and more complex societies.
 
**Neolithic Period (New Stone Age)**  
The Neolithic period brought the biggest change in human history: agriculture. Humans began domesticating plants and animals, first in areas like the Fertile Crescent. Farming allowed people to settle permanently, leading to population growth and villages.
 
New technologies appeared, such as pottery, weaving, and early metal tools. Labor became specialized, and social classes began to form. Religion also changed, with rituals and structures tied to farming cycles. By about 3000 BCE, some farming villages grew into the first civilizations with cities, governments, and writing.￼
 
**Jared Diamond: Agriculture as a “Mistake”**  
In “The Worst Mistake in the History of the Human Race,” Jared Diamond challenges the idea that agriculture made life better. He argues that hunter‑gatherers were often healthier and happier than early farmers. They worked fewer hours, ate more varied diets, and had fewer diseases.
 
According to Diamond, agriculture caused several major problems:

- Poor nutrition due to reliance on a few crops
- More disease because people lived closely together
- Social inequality, as food surpluses allowed elites to control resources
- Increased gender inequality, with women doing harder labor and having more frequent pregnancies
 
Diamond believes humans did not choose agriculture because it improved life, but because growing populations forced the change. Farming supported more people, even though overall quality of life declined.
 
**Big Picture Takeaway**  
Week 01 focused on how humans lived for most of history as hunter‑gatherers, then slowly transitioned to farming and settled life. While agriculture made civilization possible, it also introduced inequality, disease, and new struggles. Prehistory shows that progress is complicated, and that changes that help populations grow do not always improve everyday life.
