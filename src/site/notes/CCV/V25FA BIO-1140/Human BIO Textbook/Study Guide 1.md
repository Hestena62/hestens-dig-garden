# Abnormal Immune Function and Responses Study Guide

## Quiz: Short-Answer Questions

_Instructions: Answer the following questions in 2-3 complete sentences based on the provided source material._

1. Describe the role of Immunoglobulin E (IgE), mast cells, and histamines in an allergic reaction.
2. What is anaphylactic shock, and how does an EpiPen (epinephrine) counteract its effects?
3. Define autoimmune disease and provide one specific example described in the sources, including the tissue that is attacked.
4. Explain the primary difference between humoral immunity and cell-mediated immunity, identifying the main lymphocyte type responsible for each.
5. What is the function of a cytotoxic T-cell, and how does it use perforin to destroy target cells?
6. How do antigen-presenting cells (APCs) and Helper T-cells interact, and what is the role of MHC class II markers in this process?
7. Describe the basic structure of an antibody, including its four polypeptide chains and the function of its variable region.
8. What is clonal selection and why is it important for mounting an effective immune response?
9. Contrast the primary and secondary immune responses in terms of speed and magnitude of antibody production.
10. Define immunodeficiency and give an example of a disease that causes it, explaining how the immune system is compromised.

## Answer Key

1. In an allergic reaction, B cells (as plasma cells) secrete Immunoglobulin E (IgE) antibodies in response to an allergen like pollen. Excess IgE antibodies attach to mast cells, which are common in connective tissue. When the allergen later binds to this IgE on the mast cell, it causes the cell to degranulate, releasing histamines that trigger an inflammatory response characterized by redness, itching, and swelling.
2. Anaphylactic shock is a severe, life-threatening allergic reaction where a widespread release of histamines causes an abnormal dilation of circulatory blood vessels. This leads to a major drop in blood pressure that can be fatal within minutes. An EpiPen contains epinephrine, which counteracts these effects by constricting blood vessels and increasing breathing, buying time for emergency medical help to arrive.
3. An autoimmune disease is a condition where the body's own immune system, particularly white blood cells, mistakenly attacks its own normal, healthy tissue. One example is rheumatoid arthritis, where the immune system attacks cartilage cells in the joints, causing painful inflammation and damage. Another example is Type 1 diabetes, where the immune system destroys the insulin-producing beta cells in the pancreas.
4. The primary difference lies in the target and mechanism. Humoral immunity, carried out by B cells, involves the secretion of antibodies into the body's fluids (humors) to attack free, extracellular pathogens. Cell-mediated immunity, carried out primarily by T cells, targets the body's own cells that have become infected with intracellular pathogens (like viruses) or have become cancerous.
5. A cytotoxic T-cell (or killer T-cell) is responsible for destroying the body's own infected or cancerous cells. It identifies these target cells via their MHC class I markers displaying foreign proteins. The cytotoxic T-cell then releases a chemical called perforin, which forms pores in the target cell's membrane, causing fluids and solutes to enter, which leads to the cell lysing (bursting) and dying.
6. An antigen-presenting cell (APC), such as a macrophage, engulfs a pathogen and displays fragments of it on its MHC class II markers. A Helper T-cell recognizes this complex, becomes activated, and releases chemical signals called cytokines (like interleukin-2). These signals then alert and call other lymphocytes, like B cells and cytotoxic T-cells, to the site of infection, amplifying the overall immune response.
7. An antibody is a Y-shaped protein composed of four polypeptide chains: two identical large "heavy chains" and two identical smaller "light chains," held together by disulfide bridges. The ends of the Y-arms, formed by a portion of both the heavy and light chains, constitute the variable region. This region serves as the antigen-binding site, which has a specific shape to bind to a particular antigen's epitome.
8. Clonal selection is the process where a B or T cell with a receptor that specifically matches a foreign antigen is "selected" and stimulated to divide rapidly by mitosis. This creates a large population, or clone, of identical cells all specific to that one antigen. This is crucial for amplifying the very few initial lymphocytes that can recognize a specific pathogen into a large army of effector and memory cells capable of fighting the infection.
9. The primary immune response occurs upon first exposure to an antigen; it is slow, taking several days to two weeks for antibody levels to rise, during which time the individual gets sick. The secondary immune response occurs upon subsequent exposure to the same antigen; it is much faster and greater in magnitude due to the presence of memory cells, resulting in a rapid surge of antibodies that prevents sickness.
10. Immunodeficiency is a state where the immune system is deficient or compromised, leaving an individual vulnerable to any possible pathogen. An example is SCID (Severe Combined Immune Deficiency), a genetic disorder where the body fails to produce functioning lymphocytes. Another example is AIDS (Acquired Immune Deficiency Syndrome), which is caused by the HIV virus infecting and destroying Helper T-cells, crippling the immune system's ability to coordinate a response.

## Glossary of Key Terms

|   |   |
|---|---|
|Term|Definition|
|**Active Immunity**|Immunity that occurs when an individual produces their own antibodies against an antigen. It is long-lived due to the presence of memory cells. This can be acquired naturally by getting sick or artificially through vaccination.|
|**Adaptive Immunity**|A specific immune defense that develops after exposure to an antigen, involving B cells and T cells. It is characterized by specificity, diversity, and memory.|
|**Agglutination**|The clumping together of pathogens, such as bacteria or viruses, caused by antibodies. This makes it easier for phagocytes to engulf and destroy them.|
|**AIDS (Acquired Immune Deficiency Syndrome)**|The disease that results from infection by the HIV virus. It is characterized by the destruction of Helper T-cells, which severely compromises the immune system and makes the individual susceptible to opportunistic infections.|
|**Allergen**|A substance, such as pollen or dust mites, that is normally harmless but invokes an allergic (inflammatory) reaction in some individuals.|
|**Allergy**|An inflammatory reaction to a harmless foreign substance (an allergen). Symptoms can range from minor (red eyes, runny nose) to major (anaphylactic shock).|
|**Anaphylactic Shock**|A widespread, severe, and potentially fatal allergic reaction where a massive release of histamines causes extreme dilation of blood vessels, leading to a rapid and dangerous drop in blood pressure.|
|**Antibody (Immunoglobulin)**|A Y-shaped protein produced by plasma cells (a type of B cell) that circulates in body fluids. Antibodies specifically bind to foreign antigens to neutralize them or mark them for destruction.|
|**Antigen**|A foreign molecule, typically a protein or polysaccharide on the surface of a pathogen or cell, that elicits an immune response. The entire pathogen itself can also be referred to as an antigen.|
|**Antigen-Presenting Cell (APC)**|A type of white blood cell (like a macrophage) that engulfs a pathogen, breaks it down, and displays fragments of the antigen on its MHC class II markers to present to Helper T-cells.|
|**Autoimmune Disease**|A condition where the body's immune system fails to distinguish self from non-self and attacks its own healthy cells and tissues. Examples include lupus, rheumatoid arthritis, Type 1 diabetes, and multiple sclerosis.|
|**B Cell (B lymphocyte)**|A type of lymphocyte responsible for humoral immunity. B cells mature in the bone marrow, and when activated, they differentiate into plasma cells that secrete antibodies and memory cells.|
|**Cell-Mediated Immunity**|The branch of adaptive immunity that involves T cells attacking the body's own infected or cancerous cells. It is a direct cell-to-cell contact response against intracellular pathogens.|
|**Clonal Selection**|The process by which an antigen selects a specific lymphocyte (B or T cell) that has receptors matching it, causing that lymphocyte to proliferate and form a clone of identical cells to fight the infection.|
|**Complement System**|A group of proteins circulating in the blood plasma that can be activated by antibodies bound to a pathogen. Once activated, they can form a membrane attack complex that creates pores in the pathogen's membrane, causing it to lyse.|
|**Cytokines**|Chemicals secreted by immune cells, such as Helper T-cells, that act as signals to call other immune cells to an area and regulate the immune response. Interleukins are a type of cytokine.|
|**Cytotoxic T Cell (Killer T Cell)**|A type of T cell that is the key player in cell-mediated immunity. It identifies and kills the body's own cells that are infected with viruses or have become cancerous by releasing perforin.|
|**Effector Cells**|The cells that actively carry out the immune response. In humoral immunity, these are plasma cells secreting antibodies. In cell-mediated immunity, these are cytotoxic T-cells killing infected cells.|
|**Epinephrine (EpiPen)**|A hormone used in an emergency injection (like an EpiPen) to treat anaphylactic shock. It reverses the effects of histamine by constricting blood vessels and improving breathing.|
|**Helper T Cell**|A type of T cell that is central to the adaptive immune response. It becomes activated by antigen-presenting cells and then secretes cytokines to stimulate and call B cells and cytotoxic T-cells to the site of infection.|
|**Histamine**|A chemical released by mast cells and basophils during an allergic reaction. It causes vasodilation (widening of blood vessels) and increased permeability, leading to the symptoms of inflammation like swelling, redness, and itching.|
|**HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus)**|The virus that causes AIDS. It specifically infects and destroys Helper T-cells, thereby crippling the immune system's ability to coordinate a defense against pathogens.|
|**Humoral Immunity**|The branch of adaptive immunity mediated by B cells and the antibodies they secrete into the body's fluids (humors). It primarily targets free pathogens circulating outside of cells.|
|**Immunity**|The body's ability to resist a particular disease or pathogen, especially through the presence of memory cells and antibodies from a previous exposure or vaccination.|
|**Immunodeficiency**|A condition where the immune system's ability to fight infectious disease is compromised or entirely absent. It can be inherited (e.g., SCID) or acquired (e.g., AIDS).|
|**Inflammatory Response**|A nonspecific defense mechanism triggered by tissue damage or infection. It is characterized by redness, heat, swelling, and pain, and involves chemicals like histamines that increase blood flow and allow white blood cells to reach the affected area.|
|**Innate Immunity**|The body's nonspecific defenses that act rapidly against any pathogen without prior exposure. It includes physical barriers (skin, mucous membranes) and internal defenses (phagocytes, inflammation).|
|**Interferons**|Proteins secreted by virus-infected cells that can help other, non-infected cells resist viral infection.|
|**Interleukins**|A specific type of cytokine. Interleukin-1 can stimulate a Helper T-cell to divide, while Interleukin-2 is secreted by Helper T-cells to call B cells and cytotoxic T-cells to an area.|
|**Lymph**|The fluid inside lymphatic vessels. It originates as tissue fluid that has leaked from blood capillaries and contains water, small proteins, and potentially pathogens.|
|**Lymph Nodes**|Small, bean-shaped structures distributed along lymphatic vessels that filter lymph. They are packed with lymphocytes and macrophages that attack and destroy pathogens present in the lymph.|
|**Lymphatic System**|A network of vessels, nodes, and organs that runs parallel to the circulatory system. Its functions include returning excess tissue fluid to the blood and playing a key role in defending the body against pathogens.|
|**Lymphocyte**|A type of white blood cell that is the key component of the adaptive immune system. The two main types are B cells and T cells.|
|**Macrophage**|A large phagocytic white blood cell that engulfs and digests pathogens, cellular debris, and foreign substances. It can also function as an antigen-presenting cell.|
|**Major Histocompatibility Complex (MHC)**|A set of proteins found on the surface of cells that act as a unique "biochemical fingerprint" for an individual, allowing the immune system to distinguish self from non-self. Class I MHC is on all nucleated body cells, while Class II MHC is on antigen-presenting cells.|
|**Mast Cell**|A type of cell found in connective tissue that contains granules rich in histamine. In an allergic reaction, IgE antibodies bind to mast cells, causing them to degranulate and release histamine.|
|**Memory Cells**|Long-lived B and T lymphocytes that "remember" a specific antigen. They remain in circulation after a primary infection is cleared and can mount a rapid and effective secondary immune response upon re-exposure.|
|**Monoclonal Antibodies**|A collection of identical antibodies produced by a clone of B cells grown in a lab culture. Because they are highly specific to a single antigen, they have applications in medicine, such as cancer treatment.|
|**Multiple Sclerosis (MS)**|An autoimmune disease in which T cells attack the myelin sheath that insulates neurons, leading to chronic neurological disease.|
|**Neutralization**|A mechanism by which antibodies block the activity of an antigen. For example, an antibody can bind to a protein that a virus uses to infect a host cell, thereby preventing infection.|
|**Passive Immunity**|Short-term immunity resulting from the acquisition of antibodies from another source, rather than producing them oneself. It can be acquired naturally (mother to baby via breast milk) or artificially (an injection of antibodies).|
|**Pathogen**|A disease-causing agent, such as a bacterium, virus, fungus, or protozoan.|
|**Phagocytosis**|The process by which a cell, such as a macrophage or neutrophil, engulfs a large particle, such as a bacterium or cellular debris, into a vesicle to be digested internally.|
|**Plasma Cell**|A fully differentiated B cell that is a highly effective "antibody factory." Plasma cells are enlarged and produce and secrete large quantities of antibodies into the blood and lymph.|
|**Primary Immune Response**|The immune system's response to its first encounter with a specific antigen. It is relatively slow, taking several days to develop, during which the individual may experience symptoms of illness.|
|**Rheumatoid Arthritis**|An autoimmune disease characterized by a painful inflammatory response where the immune system attacks the cartilage in joints.|
|**SCID (Severe Combined Immune Deficiency)**|A group of rare, inherited disorders that cause major abnormalities of the immune system. Individuals lack functioning T and B lymphocytes, making them extremely vulnerable to infectious diseases.|
|**Secondary Immune Response**|The immune system's rapid and enhanced response to a subsequent encounter with a specific antigen. It is mediated by memory cells and is so effective that it typically prevents illness.|
|**Suppressor T Cell**|A type of T lymphocyte that produces suppressor proteins to inhibit the activity of cytotoxic T-cells and plasma cells, thereby calming the immune response after an infection has been cleared.|
|**T Cell (T lymphocyte)**|A type of lymphocyte responsible for cell-mediated immunity. T cells originate in the bone marrow but mature in the thymus gland. The main types are cytotoxic T-cells and helper T-cells.|
|**Thymus**|A lymphatic organ located above the heart where T cells mature and are "tested" to ensure they are competent and do not attack the body's own cells.|
|**Vaccination**|The administration of a vaccine (containing weakened or killed pathogens, or their surface proteins) to stimulate a primary immune response and produce memory cells, thereby providing artificially acquired active immunity against a disease.|

_Classes of Antibodies_

|   |   |   |
|---|---|---|
|Class|Structure|Presence & Function|
|**IgG**|Monomer|Main antibody type in circulation; crosses the placenta from mother to fetus; binds to pathogens, activates complement, and enhances phagocytosis.|
|**IgM**|Pentamer|Found in circulation; largest antibody; first antibody to appear in an infection; activates complement and clumps cells.|
|**IgA**|Dimer|Main antibody type in secretions such as saliva, tears, and milk; prevents pathogens from attaching to epithelial cells in digestive and respiratory tracts.|
|**IgD**|Monomer|Found on the surface of immature B cells; signifies the readiness of the B cell.|
|**IgE**|Monomer|Found as antigen receptors on mast cells in tissues; responsible for immediate allergic responses and protection against certain parasitic worms.|
